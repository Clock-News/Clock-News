The Moon: Earth's Mysterious Companion

The Moon, Earth’s only natural satellite, has fascinated humanity for centuries. It is about 3,474 kilometers in diameter—roughly one-quarter the size of Earth—and orbits our planet at an average distance of 384,400 kilometers.

The Moon has no atmosphere, which means its surface is exposed to space, leading to extreme temperatures and visible craters from asteroid impacts. Its gravitational pull causes ocean tides on Earth, a force that also slowly slows down Earth’s rotation over time.

The Moon goes through regular phases—from new moon to full moon—based on its position relative to Earth and the Sun. These phases have shaped calendars, myths, and traditions across cultures.

In 1969, the Moon became the first celestial body visited by humans, when NASA’s Apollo 11 mission landed astronauts Neil Armstrong and Buzz Aldrin on its surface. Since then, scientists have studied lunar rocks and explored the Moon’s potential for future space missions.

Though lifeless, the Moon continues to inspire curiosity, representing both a scientific frontier and a symbol of exploration beyond our world.
